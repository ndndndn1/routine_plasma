## Keyword match (>=2 required)
1. Inverse Problem Solving — The paper explicitly frames stellarator design as an inverse problem: given desired plasma properties (rotational transform iota, magnetic-well depth, elongation, QS residual, etc.), infer boundary-shape design parameters (Fourier modes of the axis and near-axis expansion coefficients).
2. Surrogate Modeling — A fast deep-learning surrogate replaces the near-axis MHD solver: the forward surrogate outputs device figures of merit from geometry; the inverse MDN samples plausible geometries from target figures of merit.
3. (Invertible Neural Networks, adjacent) — The authors use Mixture Density Networks (MDN) which, like INNs, address non-uniqueness of the inverse map by producing a distribution over design parameters conditional on the desired outputs.

## Methods (summary)
- Physics model: near-axis expansion of ideal-MHD equilibrium (Garren-Boozer / Landreman-Sengupta), which reduces stellarator equilibrium to a set of 1D ODEs along the magnetic axis. Inputs: axis Fourier modes R_n, Z_n, rotational-transform iota, eta_bar, B2c, ... Outputs: equilibrium figures of merit (iota on axis, magnetic well, |B| harmonics, Mercier stability, particle confinement proxy, elongation, ...).
- Dataset: millions of near-axis configurations generated by scanning design parameters and evaluating the near-axis solver (pyQSC/pyQIC). Each sample = (x: design parameters, y: figures of merit).
- Forward surrogate: fully-connected network y_hat = f_theta(x) trained on the simulated dataset to replicate the near-axis solver at a fraction of the cost.
- Inverse model (MDN): p(x | y_target) modelled as a mixture of K Gaussians whose means, diagonal covariances and weights are output by a neural network given y_target. This captures that several distinct stellarator geometries can meet the same target (non-unique inverse).
- Training loss for the MDN: negative log-likelihood of true design parameters x under the mixture p(x | y) — this is the standard Bishop MDN loss.
- Evaluation: sample candidates from p(x | y_target), push them through the forward surrogate and through the full near-axis solver, check that the achieved figures of merit match the target and that samples span diverse regions of the design space.

## Conclusions (as stated by the authors)
- The MDN successfully generates stellarator configurations that hit targeted properties (rotational transform, magnetic well, elongation, quasi-symmetry residual), including configurations that were not in the training set.
- Because the forward problem is ambiguous (many design parameters -> similar y), a unimodal regressor fails; the mixture output is required to represent all valid pre-images.
- The learned surrogate is orders of magnitude faster than the near-axis solver, enabling interactive design exploration and as a warm-start for classical optimisers.
- Implication for plasma reconstruction/mapping: the same conditional-density paradigm is directly applicable to non-invasive plasma diagnostics where indirect measurements constrain but do not uniquely determine the underlying plasma state; an MDN/INN provides calibrated posterior samples rather than a single brittle point estimate.

## Code availability from the authors
Not explicitly released as a single repository with the paper. Near-axis stellarator solvers used in the community are publicly available (pyQSC, pyQIC). We provide a self-contained reference implementation of the MDN inverse-design idea, with a small toy forward model, so the pipeline is reproducible without the full stellarator solver.

## Reference code
See `reference_code.py`. It:
- implements a toy "plasma-like" forward model f(x) -> y (smooth non-linear map with known ambiguity);
- trains a forward surrogate (MLP) on simulated (x, y) pairs;
- trains a Mixture Density Network p(x | y_target) with K Gaussian components using Bishop's NLL loss;
- for a test target y*, draws candidate xs from the MDN, pushes them through the forward model, and reports achieved-vs-target error and diversity across the K modes.

Run: `python reference_code.py` (PyTorch required).
